\title{Consistency-based Merging of Multiple Variability Models: Generalization and Empirical Evaluation}

\author[1]{Manuel Mörtl}[email=manuel.moertl@tugraz.at]
\author[1]{Alexander Felfernig}[email=alexander.felfernig@ist.tugraz.at]

\address[1]{Graz University of Technology, Austria}
\begin{abstract}
Integrating independently maintained variability models into a single
knowledge base is a recurring challenge when regional markets impose different
requirements on configurable systems. Such an integration requires that the
merged model preserves semantics by admitting exactly the union of the
individual solution spaces.
Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} introduced a consistency-based merge procedure for
two variability models represented as constraint satisfaction problems. In
this paper, we generalize their procedure to an arbitrary number of input
models ($n \ge 2$) and illustrate it on a three-model automotive example.
The generalized procedure produces a compact integrated knowledge base
through contextualization, consistency-based decontextualization, and
redundancy elimination. We further evaluate the procedure on industrial-scale
variability models and report observations on redundancy elimination and
contextualization behavior across varying degrees of overlap between input
models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Globally operating enterprises that sell configurable products and services
frequently maintain region-specific variability models to account for local
regulations, market conditions, or product
adaptations~\cite{Felfernig2014,Stumptner1997}. When such organizations seek
to centralize their variability management, the independently developed models
and underlying knowledge bases must be integrated. A key requirement for this
integration is semantics preservation: the merged model must admit exactly the
union of the individual solution spaces, neither introducing spurious
configurations nor losing valid ones.

Feature models~\cite{Kang1990,Czarnecki2005} are widely used to represent the
variability of configurable systems. To support automated reasoning about valid
configurations, feature models and other variability models are commonly
formalized as constraint satisfaction problems
(CSPs)~\cite{Benavides2005,Tsang1993}. The automated analysis of feature
models formalized in this way has been the subject of extensive
research~\cite{Benavides2010}.

The integration of variability models has been approached from multiple
perspectives (see Bischoff et al.~\cite{Bischoff2019} for a systematic
survey). At the level of general logical frameworks, consistency-based
merging~\cite{Delgrande2007} and arbitration
operators~\cite{Liberatore1998} have been proposed for combining knowledge
bases, but these approaches operate on arbitrary formula sets and do not
exploit the hierarchical structure inherent to variability models.
At the feature-model level, van den Broek et al.~\cite{Broek2010} and Segura
et al.~\cite{Segura2007} propose rule-based and graph-transformation-based
methods for combining feature models. However, these methods are restricted to
a fixed set of cross-tree constraint types (\emph{requires},
\emph{excludes}) and do not address redundancy in the integrated model. Acher
et al.~\cite{Acher2010} introduce composition operators for feature models,
but their merge semantics only guarantees that the merged solution space is a
superset of the union of the original spaces, not that it is
equal. The stricter union semantics formalized by Schobbens et al.~\cite{Schobbens2006}, which requires
that the merged model admits exactly the union of the original configuration
spaces, is the property that our approach implements. Contextual
modeling~\cite{Felfernig2000} provides a complementary structuring mechanism
for decentralized development and maintenance of configuration knowledge bases
and serves as a foundation for the contextualization step in the merge
procedure.

Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} address variability model integration with a
consistency-based merge procedure for two knowledge bases represented as CSPs.
Their approach contextualizes each input model with a contextual variable,
constructs the union of the contextualized knowledge bases, removes unnecessary
contextual guards through consistency checking, and eliminates redundant
constraints. The resulting integrated knowledge base is compact and its solution
space equals the union of the original solution spaces. Compared to structural
merging approaches, the procedure handles arbitrary constraint types. However,
the work is limited in two respects: it considers only the merge of two input
models, and its evaluation relies entirely on synthesized knowledge bases. The
applicability of the approach to industrial-scale real-world variability models
remains an open question, as noted by the authors themselves.
Tran~\cite{Tran2024} extends this line of work with a performance analysis
that applies the merge procedure to real-world feature models, but does not
address the generalization to multiple input models or analyze merge behavior
across models with varying structural overlap.

This paper extends the work of Uta et al.\ in two directions. First, we
generalize the merge procedure from two to an arbitrary number of consistent
input models ($n \ge 2$) that share a common vocabulary for overlapping features
but may contain features specific to a subset of models. To preserve
solution-space semantics, the generalized procedure introduces region-based
exclusion constraints that prevent subset-specific features from becoming
unconstrained in regions where they are not defined. We illustrate the
generalized procedure on an automotive example with three regional knowledge
bases.

Second, we apply the merge procedure to industrial-scale variability models
from the financial services domain~\cite{uvlhub2024} to examine whether the
approach is applicable to realistic, independently maintained knowledge bases.
Using pairwise merges of nine models, each containing more than 700 features
and 2\,000 non-structural constraints, we validate semantics preservation for
every merge and report observations on redundancy elimination and
contextualization behavior across varying degrees of structural overlap.

The contributions of this paper are: (1)~a generalization of the
consistency-based merge procedure of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} from two to $n$
variability models, (2)~a detailed illustration of the generalized procedure on
a three-model automotive example, and (3)~an empirical evaluation on
industrial-scale real-world models that addresses a central limitation of the
original work.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:example} introduces the automotive example.
Section~\ref{sec:merge} describes the generalized merge procedure.
Section~\ref{sec:realworld} presents the empirical evaluation on
industrial-scale models. Section~5 discusses threats to validity, and Section~6
concludes the paper.


\section{Example Variability Models}
\label{sec:example}
The following example extends the automotive example of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} from two to three regional knowledge bases in order to illustrate the multi-model merge procedure.

We consider three regional knowledge bases from the United States, Germany, and Oceania. Each knowledge base is represented as a constraint satisfaction problem (CSP)~\cite{Tsang1993}, that is, as a set of variables with associated domains and constraints. In this representation, variables represent features or feature properties and valid configurations correspond to CSP solutions. As in~\cite{Uta2019}, we assume that the individual knowledge bases are consistent.

The three knowledge bases share a common vocabulary for overlapping features that captures the main characteristics of the vehicle. Table~\ref{tab:example-features} lists the variables and their admissible domain values that occur in all three knowledge bases. This shared vocabulary provides the basis for alignment and comparability between regions.

The United States and Germany models share the same feature hierarchy, whereas the Oceania model introduces an additional optional feature \textit{SurfPackage} below the root feature \textit{Car}. This structural asymmetry illustrates how the merge procedure handles region-specific structural extensions while preserving solution-space semantics.

\section{Merging Multiple Variability Models}
\label{sec:merge}
This section describes the semantics-preserving generalization of the consistency-based merge procedure of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} from two to an arbitrary number of consistent regional knowledge bases ($n \ge 2$). As in the original approach, we assume that the overlapping features are represented using a shared vocabulary.
The goal is to construct a single integrated knowledge base whose solution space corresponds exactly to the union of the solution spaces of the regional knowledge bases. 

To achieve this, each regional knowledge base $\mathrm{CKB}_i$ is contextualized by introducing a contextual variable \textit{region}. Each constraint of $\mathrm{CKB}_i$ is then guarded by the corresponding region value, resulting in a contextualized knowledge base $\mathrm{CKB}'_i$. This step isolates regional semantics within a unified model and prevents unintended interactions between constraints originating from different regional knowledge bases.

The contextualized knowledge bases are then combined by taking their union $\mathrm{CKB}' = \bigcup_{i=1}^{n} \mathrm{CKB}'_i$. 
For each feature $f$ that occurs only in a subset of the regional knowledge bases, an implication $region = r_i \rightarrow f = no$ is introduced for each knowledge base $\mathrm{CKB}'_i$ in which $f$ is not defined. 
This prevents the union model from admitting configurations in which features are unconstrained in regions where they do not occur, and is therefore necessary for semantics preservation.

After constructing the union model, the merge procedure performs consistency-based decontextualization. For each contextualized constraint $c'$, let $c$ denote its decontextualized form obtained by removing the region guard. The procedure tests whether $c$ is entailed by the current intermediate merge state by checking if $\{\neg c\} \cup \mathrm{CKB}' \cup \mathrm{CKB}$ is inconsistent. If this is the case, no configuration satisfying $\neg c$ exists and $c$ can be added to the integrated knowledge base in decontextualized form. Otherwise, the contextualized constraint $c'$ is retained. Hence, contextual guards are removed only when they are not required for semantics preservation.

In a second phase, redundant constraints are removed by testing for each constraint $c \in \mathrm{CKB}$, if $\neg c$ is inconsistent with the merged knowledge base $\mathrm{CKB} - \{c\}$. In this case, $c$ is logically implied by the remaining merged knowledge base and can be removed. This yields a more compact integrated knowledge base without changing its solution space.

The Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge-algorithm} summarizes the generalized merge procedure.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{CKB-MULTI-MERGE($\mathrm{CKB}'_{1}, \ldots, \mathrm{CKB}'_{n}$): $\mathrm{CKB}$}
\label{alg:merge-algorithm}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}l@{\hspace{0.8em}}l@{}}
1:  & $\{\mathrm{CKB}'_{1}, \ldots, \mathrm{CKB}'_{n}\}$: $n \ge 2$ contextualized and consistent regional knowledge bases \\
2:  & $\{\, c' : \text{a contextualized version of constraint } c \,\}$ \\
3:  & $\{\, r_i : \text{value of the contextual variable } \textit{region} \text{ associated with knowledge base } i \,\}$ \\
4:  & $\{\, \mathrm{CKB} : \text{knowledge base resulting from the merge operation} \,\}$ \\
5:  & $\mathrm{CKB} \leftarrow \emptyset;$ \\
6:  & $\mathrm{CKB}' \leftarrow \bigcup_{i=1}^{n} \mathrm{CKB}'_{i};$ \\
7:  & \textbf{for all} features $f$ that are not defined in all $\mathrm{CKB}'_{i}$ \textbf{do} \\
8:  & \quad \textbf{for all} $i$ \textbf{such that} $f$ is not defined in region $r_i$ \textbf{do} \\
9:  & \qquad $\mathrm{CKB}' \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB}' \cup \{\, \mathrm{region} = r_i \rightarrow f = no \,\};$ \\
10: & \quad \textbf{end for} \\
11: & \textbf{end for} \\
12: & \textbf{for all} $c' \in \mathrm{CKB}'$ \textbf{do} \\
13: & \quad \textbf{if} $\text{inconsistent}(\{\neg c\} \cup \mathrm{CKB}' \cup \mathrm{CKB})$ \textbf{then} \\
14: & \quad\quad $\mathrm{CKB} \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB} \cup \{c\};$ \\
15: & \quad \textbf{else} \\
16: & \quad\quad $\mathrm{CKB} \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB} \cup \{c'\};$ \\
17: & \quad \textbf{end if} \\
18: & \quad $\mathrm{CKB}' \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB}' - \{c'\};$ \\
19: & \textbf{end for} \\
20: & \textbf{for all} $c \in \mathrm{CKB}$ \textbf{do} \\
21: & \quad \textbf{if} \ \text{inconsistent}(($\mathrm{CKB} - \{c\}) \cup \{\neg c\}$) \textbf{then} \\
22: & \quad\quad $\mathrm{CKB} \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB} - \{c\};$ \\
23: & \quad \textbf{end if} \\
24: & \textbf{end for} \\
25: & \textbf{return} \ $\mathrm{CKB};$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{algorithm}

For the three regional knowledge bases (US, GER and OCE), the
merge introduces a mandatory \textit{region} choice under the root
feature \textit{Car} and excludes the Oceania-specific feature
\textit{SurfPackage} from regions other than \textit{OCE}.

\section{Empirical Evaluation on Industrial-Scale Variability Models}
\label{sec:realworld}
In contrast to the evaluation by Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019}, which relies on synthesized variability models, we investigate the behavior of the merge procedure on industrial-scale variability models from the financial services domain. The models were obtained from a publicly available UVL repository~\cite{uvlhub2024} and represent variants of the same configuration domain, which we treat as independently maintained knowledge bases for the purposes of this evaluation. Each model contains more than 700 features and more than 2{,}000 non-structural constraints, providing an industrial-scale setting for analyzing redundancy elimination and contextualization effects. To observe these effects across different degrees of structural overlap, we performed pairwise merges of all nine models.\footnote{The merge procedure and the corresponding consistency checks were implemented in Java using the Choco Solver – www.choco-solver.org.}

\subsection{Structural Similarity of the Finance Models}
To interpret the results of the pairwise merges, we first characterize
the structural similarity of the nine finance models in terms of feature-set overlap.
All input models share a common vocabulary over overlapping features, but differ in their full feature sets, hierarchy structures, and non-structural constraints. 
Table~\ref{tab:finance_similarity} summarizes the model sizes and the pairwise Jaccard similarity of their feature sets. The similarity matrix suggests a separation into two groups. Finance~1--Finance~4 have a very high pairwise overlap, while Finance~5--Finance~9 also show a high overlap among themselves but a substantially lower overlap with Finance~1--Finance ~ 4.

\subsection{Redundancy Elimination and Contextualization Effects}
Our evaluation examines two outcomes of the consistency-based merge on the finance models: (i) the reduction of redundant non-structural constraints through redundancy elimination, and (ii) the share of constraints that remain contextualized in the integrated model.

Figure~\ref{fig:finance-contextualization-overlap} relates both outcomes to the feature-set overlap of the input models for all pairwise merges. 
A shared feature space is a precondition for both redundancy elimination and contextualization, since constraints can only coincide or conflict where they refer to shared features. However, the results do not indicate that feature-set overlap determines either outcome. Instead, both are driven by the non-structural constraints defined over the shared feature space: redundancy elimination depends on whether constraints become logically redundant, whereas contextualization depends on whether constraints over shared features are globally compatible.

\subsection{Validation of Semantics Preservation}
For each pairwise merge, we validated semantics preservation by checking whether
\[
\mathrm{Sol}(CKB_{Merge}) = \mathrm{Sol}(CKB_1) \cup \mathrm{Sol}(CKB_2).
\]
In this formulation, each knowledge base is interpreted as the conjunction of its constraints.
This was verified by confirming that
\[
CKB_{Merge} \land \neg (CKB_1 \lor CKB_2)
\]
and
\[
\neg CKB_{Merge} \land (CKB_1 \lor CKB_2)
\]
are both unsatisfiable.
The first condition checks that the integrated knowledge base does not admit additional configurations, and the second condition checks that no valid configuration of the input knowledge bases is lost.


\section{Threats to Validity}
The main threat to external validity is the representativeness of the models used in our evaluation. The automotive example is synthetic and serves only to illustrate and validate the merge procedure. The empirical analysis is based on real-world variability models from the financial services domain obtained from a publicly available repository. However, these models originate from a single domain and may not be representative of variability models in other application domains. 
A threat to internal validity is that the merge procedure of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} was independently re-implemented. This may introduce implementation-specific differences, for example, in preprocessing, solver behavior, or runtime characteristics, even if the resulting solution spaces are preserved.
Furthermore, the evaluation relies on derived metrics such as feature-set overlap, contextualization share, and reduction of non-structural constraints. These measures capture observable aspects of merge behavior but do not directly quantify semantic similarity between models.


\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
In this paper, we generalized the consistency-based merging approach of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} from two to an arbitrary number of variability models while preserving semantics. The proposed procedure constructs a compact integrated knowledge base through contextualization, consistency-based decontextualization, and redundancy elimination.

Our evaluation on industrial-scale variability models from the financial services domain shows that the approach is applicable beyond the synthesized setting of the original work.

Future work should include evaluating the approach on variability models from additional application domains in order to assess the generalizability of the observed effects and the applicability of the merge procedure. Further directions include analyzing true multi-model merges beyond pairwise combinations, developing measures that better capture semantic similarity between models, and addressing vocabulary alignment for independently developed or evolving knowledge bases.
