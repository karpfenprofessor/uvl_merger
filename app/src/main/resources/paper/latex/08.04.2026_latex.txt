\begin{abstract}
Variability models that are developed independently for different markets or organizational contexts often need to be integrated into a single knowledge base. For such an integration to be useful, the merged model must preserve semantics, that is, its solution space must correspond exactly to the union of the solution spaces of the input models. This paper extends the consistency-based merge approach of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} from two to an arbitrary number of variability models represented as constraint satisfaction problems. The generalized procedure combines contextualization, feature-region guarding for subset-specific features, consistency-based decontextualization, and elimination of redundancies to construct a compact integrated knowledge base. We illustrate the approach on an automotive example with three regional models and evaluate it on industrial-scale variability models from the financial services domain. The empirical results show that elimination of redundancies removes a substantial number of redundant non-structural constraints, especially for structurally similar model pairs, while contextualization is driven primarily by semantic incompatibilities of non-structural constraints over shared features.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Configuration~\cite{Stumptner1997,Felfernig2014} is a well-established
application area of Artificial Intelligence and is used in domains such as
telecommunications, financial services, manufacturing and software
systems. In many of these settings, configuration knowledge is represented by
variability models, for example, feature models, which provide a structured way
to capture the variability of complex systems~\cite{Kang1990,Czarnecki2005}.
To support automated reasoning over valid configurations, such models are often
formalized as constraint satisfaction problems (CSPs)~\cite{Benavides2005,Tsang1993}.
For organizations operating globally, configuration knowledge is frequently
maintained in multiple regional or organizational variants that reflect local
requirements, regulations, or product adaptations. Integrating these variants
into a single model is challenging because the merged model must represent exactly the union of the individual solution spaces: it must not introduce configurations absent from every source model, and it must not lose configurations valid in at least one..

Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} address this problem with a consistency-based merge
procedure for two variability models. Their approach relies on
contextualization and conflict detection to construct an integrated knowledge
base whose solution space is equal to the union of the solution spaces of the
original models. In addition, the procedure removes unnecessary
contextualization and logically redundant constraints, which yields a more
compact merged model. However, the original work is limited in two important
respects. First, it considers only the merge of two input models. Second, its
empirical evaluation is based on synthesized knowledge bases, while industrial-scale
variability models remain an open direction for further investigation.

This paper builds on that foundation. We generalize the
consistency-based merge procedure to support an arbitrary number of consistent
input models ($n \geq 2$) that share a common vocabulary for overlapping
features, while also allowing features that occur only in a subset of the
models. To preserve semantics in this setting, the merge procedure introduces
feature-region implications for subset-specific features, performs
consistency-based decontextualization, and removes redundant constraints from
the resulting integrated knowledge base. We illustrate the generalized
procedure on an automotive example with three regional models.

We then complement the synthesized evaluation of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019}
with an empirical study on industrial-scale real-world variability models from
the financial services domain. Using pairwise merges of nine models, we analyze
how structural overlap relates to two properties of the merge result: the
reduction of non-structural constraints during redundancy elimination and the share of
constraints that remain contextualized. The results show that high structural
overlap often coincides with stronger redundancy elimination effects, but does not by itself
explain contextualization behavior. Instead, contextualization is primarily
determined by the semantic compatibility of non-structural constraints over
shared features.

The contributions of this paper are threefold. First, we extend the
consistency-based merge procedure of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} from two to
multiple variability models. Second, we provide a detailed illustration of the
generalized merge process on a three-model automotive example. Third, we
evaluate the approach on industrial-scale models, thereby addressing
a central limitation of the original work.


\section{Example Variability Models}
\label{sec:example}
The following example extends the automotive example of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} from two to three regional knowledge bases in order to illustrate the multi-model merge procedure.

We consider three regional knowledge bases from the United States, Germany, and Oceania. Each knowledge base is represented as a constraint satisfaction problem (CSP)~\cite{Tsang1993}, that is, as a set of variables with associated domains and constraints. In this representation, variables represent features or feature properties and valid configurations correspond to CSP solutions. As in~\cite{Uta2019}, we assume that the individual knowledge bases are consistent.

The three knowledge bases share a common vocabulary for overlapping features that captures the main characteristics of the vehicle. Table~\ref{tab:example-features} lists the variables and their admissible domain values that occur in all three knowledge bases. This shared vocabulary provides the basis for alignment and comparability between regions.

The United States and Germany models share the same feature hierarchy, whereas the Oceania model introduces an additional optional feature \textit{SurfPackage} below the root feature \textit{Car}. This structural asymmetry is deliberately introduced to illustrate how the merge procedure handles region-specific structural extensions while preserving solution-space semantics.

\section{Merging Multiple Variability Models}
\label{sec:merge}
This section describes the semantics-preserving generalization of the consistency-based merge procedure of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} from two to an arbitrary number of consistent regional knowledge bases ($n \ge 2$). As in the original approach, we assume that the overlapping features are represented using a shared vocabulary.
The goal is to construct a single integrated knowledge base whose solution space corresponds exactly to the union of the solution spaces of the regional knowledge bases. 

To achieve this, each regional knowledge base $\mathrm{CKB}_i$ is contextualized by introducing a contextual variable \textit{region}. Each constraint of $\mathrm{CKB}_i$ is then guarded by the corresponding region value, resulting in a contextualized knowledge base $\mathrm{CKB}'_i$. This step isolates regional semantics within a unified model and prevents unintended interactions between constraints originating from different regional knowledge bases.

The contextualized knowledge bases are then combined by taking their union $\mathrm{CKB}' = \bigcup_{i=1}^{n} \mathrm{CKB}'_i$. For each feature $f$ that occurs only in a subset of the regional knowledge bases, an implication $f = yes \rightarrow region = r_i$ is introduced for each knowledge base $\mathrm{CKB}'_i$ in which $f$ is defined. This prevents the union model from admitting configurations that select features specific to one regional knowledge base together with a different region value, and is therefore necessary for semantics preservation.

After constructing the union model, the merge procedure performs consistency-based decontextualization. For each contextualized constraint $c'$, let $c$ denote its decontextualized form obtained by removing the region guard. The procedure tests whether $c$ is entailed by the current intermediate merge state by checking if $\{\neg c\} \cup \mathrm{CKB}' \cup \mathrm{CKB}$ is inconsistent. If this is the case, no configuration satisfying $\neg c$ exists and $c$ can be added to the integrated knowledge base in decontextualized form. Otherwise, the contextualized constraint $c'$ is retained. Hence, contextual guards are removed only when they are not required for semantics preservation.

In a second phase, redundant constraints are removed by testing for each constraint $c \in \mathrm{CKB}$, if $\neg c$ is inconsistent with the merged knowledge base $\mathrm{CKB} - \{c\}$. In this case, $c$ is logically implied by the remaining merged knowledge base and can be removed. This yields a more compact integrated knowledge base without changing its solution space.

The Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge-algorithm} summarizes the generalized merge procedure.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{CKB-MULTI-MERGE($\mathrm{CKB}'_{1}, \ldots, \mathrm{CKB}'_{n}$): $\mathrm{CKB}$}
\label{alg:merge-algorithm}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}l@{\hspace{0.8em}}l@{}}
1:  & $\{\mathrm{CKB}'_{1}, \ldots, \mathrm{CKB}'_{n}\}$: $n \ge 2$ contextualized and consistent regional  knowledge bases \\
2:  & $\{\, c' : \text{a contextualized version of constraint } c \,\}$ \\
3:  & $\{\, r_i : \text{value of the contextual variable } \textit{region} \text{ associated with knowledge base } i \,\}$ \\
4:  & $\{\, \mathrm{CKB} : \text{knowledge base resulting from the merge operation} \,\}$ \\
5:  & $\mathrm{CKB} \leftarrow \emptyset;$ \\
6:  & $\mathrm{CKB}' \leftarrow \bigcup_{i=1}^{n} \mathrm{CKB}'_{i};$ \\
7:  & \textbf{for all} features $f$ that are not defined in all $\mathrm{CKB}'_{i}$ \textbf{do} \\
8:  & \quad \textbf{for all} $i$ \textbf{such that} $f$ is defined in $\mathrm{CKB}'_{i}$ \textbf{do} \\
9:  & \qquad $\mathrm{CKB}' \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB}' \cup \{\, f = yes \rightarrow \mathrm{region} = r_i \,\};$ \\
10: & \quad \textbf{end for} \\
11: & \textbf{end for} \\
12: & \textbf{for all} $c' \in \mathrm{CKB}'$ \textbf{do} \\
13: & \quad \textbf{if} $\text{inconsistent}(\{\neg c\} \cup \mathrm{CKB}' \cup \mathrm{CKB})$ \textbf{then} \\
14: & \quad\quad $\mathrm{CKB} \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB} \cup \{c\};$ \\
15: & \quad \textbf{else} \\
16: & \quad\quad $\mathrm{CKB} \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB} \cup \{c'\};$ \\
17: & \quad \textbf{end if} \\
18: & \quad $\mathrm{CKB}' \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB}' - \{c'\};$ \\
19: & \textbf{end for} \\
20: & \textbf{for all} $c \in \mathrm{CKB}$ \textbf{do} \\
21: & \quad \textbf{if} \ \text{inconsistent}(($\mathrm{CKB} - \{c\}) \cup \{\neg c\}$) \textbf{then} \\
22: & \quad\quad $\mathrm{CKB} \leftarrow \mathrm{CKB} - \{c\};$ \\
23: & \quad \textbf{end if} \\
24: & \textbf{end for} \\
25: & \textbf{return} \ $\mathrm{CKB};$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Empirical Evaluation on Industrial-Scale Variability Models}
\label{sec:realworld}
In contrast to the evaluation by Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019}, which relies on synthesized variability models, we investigate the behavior of the merge procedure on industrial-scale variability models from the financial services domain. The models were obtained from a publicly available UVL repository~\cite{uvlhub2024} and represent variants of the same configuration domain, treated as independent knowledge bases for the purposes of this evaluation. Each model contains more than 700 features and more than 2{,}000 non-structural constraints, providing a realistic setting to analyze redundancy elimination and contextualization effects. 
To observe these effects across different degrees of structural overlap, we performed pairwise merges of all nine models.


\subsection{Structural Similarity of the Finance Models}
To interpret the results of pairwise merges, we first characterize
the structural similarity of the nine finance models in terms of feature-set overlap.
All input models share a partial common vocabulary over their overlapping features, but differ in their full feature sets, hierarchy structures, and the semantics of their non-structural constraints. 
Table~\ref{tab:finance_similarity} summarizes the model sizes and their pairwise similarity over their feature sets. 
The similarity matrix reveals two groups. Finance~1--Finance~4
show a very high mutual overlap and can be regarded as a first structurally coherent group. Finance~5--Finance~9 form a second group with high internal similarity, but substantially lower overlap with Finance~1--Finance~4.

\subsection{Redundancy Elimination and Contextualization Effects}
Our evaluation focuses on two outcomes of the
consistency-based merge: (i) the reduction of redundant non-structural constraints through redundancy elimination, and (ii) the share of constraints that remain contextualized in the merged model.
 
Figure~\ref{fig:finance-contextualization-overlap} relates these outcomes to the overlap of the feature sets of the input models. Pairs with a high degree of feature overlap often exhibit substantial reductions during redundancy elimination, which indicates that overlapping feature sets create opportunities to identify logically redundant constraints. However, similar levels of feature overlap can still lead to noticeably different reduction values, showing that overlap alone does not determine the amount of redundancy that can be eliminated.
For contextualization, the results show an even weaker dependence on feature set overlap. Model pairs with comparable overlap can exhibit substantially different contextualization shares, while low-overlap pairs do not necessarily lead to high contextualization. This can be explained by the role of non-structural constraints: contextualization is required when constraints over shared features are not globally compatible and must remain region-specific, whereas constraints that are local to a single model do not introduce conflicts and therefore do not need to stay contextualized.

\subsection{Discussion}
The evaluation indicates that feature-set overlap provides a useful structural baseline for interpreting pairwise merges but is not sufficient to explain merge outcomes.
More generally, the results highlight that merge behavior is determined by how constraints interact over the shared feature space. In particular, whether constraints can be decontextualized or must remain context-specific depends on their logical compatibility rather than on structural similarity alone.
Since the evaluation is based on finance models from a single domain, these observations should be interpreted as domain-specific evidence rather than as universally generalizable properties.
Overall, the results show that the consistency-based merge procedure is applicable to industrial-scale knowledge bases beyond the synthesized settings of the original evaluation. It can consolidate large variants by eliminating redundancy while preserving region- or variant-specific semantics.

\section{Threats to Validity}
The main threat to external validity is the representativeness of the models used in our evaluation. The automotive example is synthetic and serves only to illustrate and validate the merge procedure. The empirical analysis is based on real-world variability models from the financial services domain obtained from a publicly available repository. However, these models originate from a single domain and may not be representative of variability models in other application domains. 
A threat to internal validity is that the merge procedure of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} was independently re-implemented. This may introduce implementation-specific differences, for example, in preprocessing, solver behavior, or runtime characteristics, even if the resulting solution spaces are preserved.
Furthermore, the evaluation relies on derived metrics such as feature-set overlap, contextualization share, and reduction of non-structural constraints. These measures capture observable aspects of merge behavior but do not directly quantify semantic similarity between models.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
In this paper, we extend the consistency-based merging approach of Uta et al.~\cite{Uta2019} to support the integration of an arbitrary number of variability models while preserving semantics. The proposed procedure constructs a compact integrated knowledge base through contextualization, decontextualization, and elimination of redundancy.

Our evaluation on industrial-scale variability models from the financial services domain shows that the merge procedure can be applied to industrial-scale models and that merge outcomes are shaped by both structural overlap and semantic differences in non-structural constraints. In particular, structural overlap is a useful baseline for interpreting the merge, but it is not sufficient to explain the characteristics of the resulting integrated model.

Future work should include evaluating the approach on variability models from additional application domains in order to assess the generalizability of the observed effects and the applicability of the merge procedure. Further directions include analyzing true multi-model merges beyond pairwise combinations, developing measures that better capture semantic similarity between models, and addressing vocabulary alignment for independently developed knowledge bases.
